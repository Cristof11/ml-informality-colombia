Phase 01 · CRISP-ML

Business Understanding

According to DANE's GEIH (2024), 55.8% of all employed workers in Colombia operate informally — and the rate jumps to 84.7% inside micro-businesses. This project uses open EMICRON data to make that problem measurable and actionable.

01 · Objective

Business Objectives

  • ◆Apply K-Means clustering to the DANE EMICRON dataset to automatically segment Colombian micro-businesses into distinct informality profiles.
  • ◆Give local governments and chambers of commerce a data-driven tool to prioritize formalization efforts — moving beyond the blunt national average (55.8%) toward actionable, city-level intelligence.
  • ◆Identify which segments are closest to formalization ("gateway clusters") so that MinCIT and DNP can design targeted incentive programs with the highest return on public investment.
  • ◆Produce a reproducible pipeline that can be re-run each year as new EMICRON data is released, enabling longitudinal tracking of informality cluster evolution across Colombia.
❓
02 · Questions

Key Questions

  • ◆Which combination of variables (income, RUT, ARL, location, sector) best predicts informality level?
  • ◆Which cities show the highest concentration of high-risk clusters?
  • ◆Can we identify a "gateway to formality" profile — businesses that are informal but almost ready to formalize?
📊
03 · Data

Data Assessment

  • ◆Primary source: EMICRON 2023 (DANE) — 81,018 records. Covers micro-businesses with up to 9 employees across 24 departments and Bogotá D.C.
  • ◆The dataset includes 7 structured modules: economic activity (CIIU), entrepreneurship history, location type, workforce profile, business formalization, ICT usage, and financial inclusion.
  • ◆Complementary source: GEIH 2024 — used to validate city-level informality rates and enrich the model with regional labor market context.
  • ◆Data quality note: monthly sales field (P3088) had a ~7% missing rate in 2024 — handled via median imputation by sector and city.
⚖️
04 · Viability

Feasibility Analysis

  • ◆Technically viable: EMICRON is publicly available, annually updated, and documented with a DDI/XML data dictionary. All dependencies are open-source Python libraries.
  • ◆Privacy constraint: the dataset is anonymized by DANE — direct and pseudo-identifiers removed. Sub-municipal spatial analysis is not possible.
  • ◆Coverage gap: EMICRON does not cover 8 low-density departments (Arauca, Casanare, Vichada, Guainía, Guaviare, Vaupés, Putumayo, Amazonas).
  • ◆Ethical consideration: cluster labels must be framed as structural policy targets — not judgments on individual businesses. Findings should inform incentives, not penalties.

Why This Problem Matters

Cities with the highest informality rates (GEIH, 2023): Sincelejo (70%), Riohacha (63.9%), Valledupar (63.3%). Lowest: Bogotá (32.9%), Manizales (34.2%), Medellín (37%). This 37-point spread shows informality is highly localized — exactly the pattern K-Means clustering can detect.


Meanwhile, 93.6% of micro-business owners made no ARL contributions in 2024, leaving millions of workers completely without occupational risk protection.

📍 Cities in Scope

EMICRON covers 24 departments + Bogotá D.C., with city-level breakdowns for the 23 main metropolitan areas tracked by GEIH — including Sincelejo, Barranquilla, Cali, Medellín, and Bogotá.

🎓 Stakeholders

Municipal governments, MinCIT, Chambers of Commerce, DNP (Departamento Nacional de Planeación), academic researchers in labor economics and public policy.
